Currency conversion fees, decoded

The "free transfer" is usually paid for in the FX rate. Here's how to read what you're actually paying.

📊

Mid-market rate vs. the rate you get

The mid-market rate is the midpoint between the buy and sell price for a currency pair on the wholesale market — the rate you see on Google or Reuters. Almost no consumer or business product gives you the mid-market rate. The difference between it and the rate you actually receive is the FX margin, and it's how most providers earn on currency conversion.

A "no-fee" international transfer with a 2.5% FX margin on $10,000 costs you $250 — just not on the fee line.

How GlobalPay calculates conversion

  • The rate displayed at the moment you initiate a conversion is the rate you get.
  • That rate includes the FX margin — there is no separate conversion fee added afterward.
  • The margin varies by currency pair and account tier and is shown alongside the mid-market rate at conversion time.

When conversion actually happens

If you hold a multi-currency account, incoming funds land in the matching currency and are not auto-converted. Conversion only happens when you explicitly convert a balance, or when you withdraw to a local bank account in a currency different from the source balance.

Frequently asked questions

A currency conversion fee is the cost charged to exchange funds from one currency to another. It can take the form of a flat fee, a percentage of the converted amount, or a margin built into the exchange rate you receive.
The mid-market rate is the midpoint between the buy and sell prices of a currency pair on the wholesale foreign exchange market. It is the rate displayed on Google, Reuters, and XE, and is the benchmark against which providers' rates are compared.
GlobalPay's conversion fee is built into the exchange rate shown at the time of conversion. The rate includes a margin over the mid-market rate that varies by currency pair and account type, and no additional conversion fee is added on top.
If you hold a multi-currency GlobalPay account, incoming funds settle in the original currency and are not converted automatically. Conversion happens only when you initiate one manually, or when you withdraw to a local bank in a currency that differs from the source balance.
The exchange rate shown in your GlobalPay dashboard at the moment you confirm a conversion is the rate that will be applied to that transaction. Rates fluctuate continuously based on market conditions and are refreshed in the dashboard.
You can avoid conversion fees by keeping funds in the original currency, paying suppliers in that currency, or withdrawing to a local bank account denominated in the same currency. Conversion fees apply only when an actual currency exchange takes place.

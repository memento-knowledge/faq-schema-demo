Cross-border payments: how they actually work

From SWIFT wires to local rails — what determines speed, cost, and reliability when money crosses a border.

🌐

The two routes money can take

When you send money from one country to another, the payment travels over one of two types of networks: a global correspondent network like SWIFT, or a domestic ("local") rail such as ACH in the US, SEPA in Europe, or Faster Payments in the UK. Local rails are typically faster and cheaper, but only work when both ends are connected to that network.

Most "international transfer takes 5 days" stories come from SWIFT wires bouncing through 2–3 intermediary banks. Local rails settle the same day or next.

What you actually pay

The headline "transfer fee" is rarely the largest cost. Three things stack up:

  • Transfer fee — fixed or a percentage, charged by your provider.
  • FX margin — the spread between the mid-market rate and the rate you actually get. Often 1–3%.
  • Intermediary bank fees — deductions taken in transit on SWIFT wires.

Compliance and reporting

Cross-border payments are subject to anti-money-laundering checks, sanctions screening, and country-specific reporting. Most delays under 5 business days are caused by compliance review rather than rail speed.

Frequently asked questions

A cross-border payment is a transfer of funds where the payer and the payee are in different countries. The transaction typically passes through correspondent banks or a payment network and may involve a currency conversion before it settles in the recipient's account.
Most cross-border payments settle within 1 to 3 business days. SWIFT wires can take up to 5 business days, while payments routed over local rails such as SEPA in Europe or ACH in the United States are usually faster and complete within 1 business day.
Cross-border payments commonly carry three types of fees: a fixed or percentage transfer fee charged by the sending provider, a foreign exchange margin applied to the conversion rate, and intermediary or beneficiary bank fees deducted in transit.
GlobalPay supports sending and receiving in more than 70 currencies, including USD, EUR, GBP, JPY, CAD, AUD, CNY, HKD, SGD, AED, and MXN. The full list and availability depend on the sender and recipient country.
A SWIFT/BIC code is required for international wire transfers. If the payment is routed over a local rail such as ACH, SEPA, or Faster Payments, you only need the local account details (routing number, IBAN, or sort code) and a SWIFT code is not needed.
